# study-of-python-poop

A field-study primer and reference for the biological and educational study of python scat (scat, droppings, feces). Serious science with a wink — rigorous methods, clear notes, and a little natural-history curiosity.

---

## Overview

This repository collects protocols, observations, reference notes, and an image gallery intended to support safe, ethical, and reproducible study of python fecal material for ecological, dietary, health, and educational purposes. Content here is intended for researchers, field biologists, students, and citizen scientists interested in noninvasive indicators of python ecology.

Key goals:
- Produce clear, reproducible morphology and context notes for field identification.
- Provide guidance on safe collection and handling of fecal samples.
- Build an image reference gallery to support identification, annotation, and training of automated tools.

---

## Why it matters

Fecal material is a low-impact source of high-value biological information:
- Diet reconstruction: prey remains, hair, bone, feathers, and DNA.
- Health metrics: parasites, gut microbiome signatures, and parasite load.
- Population and movement inference: occurrence data where direct observation is difficult.
- Education and outreach: accessible way to teach field biology and noninvasive sampling.

Studying scat is often the least disruptive way to learn about a species’ ecology — and it can reveal stories that animals rarely tell themselves.

## Morphology notes

Use consistent, concise descriptors when recording scat morphology. Recommended fields for each observation:
- **Form**: tubular, segmented, paste-like, pelletized.
- **Size**: approximate length and maximum diameter (mm/cm).
- **Color**: use standardized color references or plain descriptors (dark brown, greenish, black, etc.).
- **Consistency**: firm, soft, crumbly, liquid, mucous-covered.
- **Contents**: visible hair, feathers, scales, bone fragments, insect exoskeletons, seeds.
- **Odor**: mild, pungent, noxious (record carefully — subjective).
- **Age estimate**: fresh, <24h, 1–3 days, >3 days (include environmental context).
- **Associated marks**: tracks, regurgitation, nearby prey remains.

Interpretation tips:
- Presence of feathers/fur + bone shards often indicates recent ingestion of vertebrate prey.
- Mucus or clear coating may indicate digestive disturbance or recent feeding behavior.
- Very fibrous or plant-rich content can indicate secondary ingestion (prey stomach contents).

## Habitat / Diet clues

Context is as important as the scat itself. Record:
- **Microhabitat**: under rock, in vegetation, near water, beneath logs, in burrows, on trails.
- **Macrohabitat**: forest, wetland, agricultural edge, urban fringe.
- **Time & weather**: date, time of day, temperature, precipitation (affects degradation).
- **Co-occurrences**: nests, den sites, sheds, prey carcasses, human garbage.

Diet clues to look for:
- Feather quills and barbs → bird prey.
- Fur tufts and small bones → mammals (rodents, small mammals).
- Scales, fish bones → piscivorous events (if in appropriate habitats).
- Seeds or plant fibers → secondary ingestion via prey stomach contents.

Combine morphological notes with habitat clues to produce robust dietary inferences. When in doubt, collect or swab samples for lab-based DNA/metabarcoding.

## Ethics / Safety

Field safety and ethical considerations are critical. Follow institutional guidance and local regulations.

Personal safety and sample handling:
- Always wear disposable nitrile gloves when handling scat. Use forceps or sterile scoops.
- Use face protection (mask/respirator) when aerosolization risk exists.
- Carry a small field kit: gloves, sterile vials, ethanol or appropriate preservative, zip-lock bags, labels, permanent marker, disposable scoops, disinfectant.
- Decontaminate gear between sites (10% bleach or manufacturer-recommended disinfectant), and dispose of biohazard waste appropriately.

Permits and wildlife disturbance:
- Obtain required permits for sample collection, export, or manipulative work.
- Minimize disturbance of animals and habitats; do not approach occupied dens or nests.
- Respect private land and local cultural sites — obtain permission before working on private property.

Data ethics:
- Anonymize sensitive location data for endangered or persecuted species in public outputs.
- Follow institutional biosafety and ethical review processes for sample analyses.
